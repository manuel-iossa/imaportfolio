The Victorian Age

The Victorian Age was a period of enormous transformation for Great Britain, marked by Queen Victoria, characterized by great industrial, economic and colonial expansion, but also by profound social contradictions, with poverty, exploitation and inequality alongside reforms (suffrage, compulsory education, labor laws) and an explosion of literature (the Victorian novel) and science, all under the influence of a strong bourgeois morality.

Alan Turing

Alan Turing is considered the father of the modern IT, he was born in United Kingdom.
While he studied mathematics at Cambridge then he went to the USA and he began to work on the first digital program the Turing Machine.
The Turing machine could read a series of 0's and 1's from a tape describing the steps needed to solve a problem or a task.
In 1938 he returned in the UK to help the allies in the second world war, him and other people developed a machine, the bombe, that could decode the machine, the enigma, that the nazi soldiers used to talk with each others.
He also developed the Turing test, in this test there were two person and a machine; the machine and one person had to answer via keyboard the question that the other person did, if the person who made the questions couldn't distinguish the answer of the other person from the machine's one, the machine could be called "thinker".

World War I

World War I (1914–1918), also called the Great War, was a global conflict that pitted the Allied Powers (primarily Great Britain, France, Russia, and later the United States and Italy) against the Central Powers (Germany, Austria-Hungary, and the Ottoman Empire).

War Poets

War poets refer to writers who documented the brutal realities and visceral horrors of combat through their verse.
Before the true devastation of trench warfare was widely understood, the earliest war poets wrote with a sense of romantic nationalism, duty, and noble sacrifice.
As the war dragged on, the poetry shifted sharply toward realism. Soldiers who experienced shell shock, poison gas, and artillery barrages wrote crude, visceral, and emotionally scarred verse to expose the "pity of war".

Modernist Writers

Literary modernism was an early 20th-century movement defined by a deliberate departure from traditional forms.
Writers rejected linear plots and objective realities in favor of psychological exploration, fragmentation, and "stream-of-consciousness" narratives

World War II

The Second World War (1939-1945) was the most devastating conflict in history, which divided the world between the Axis Powers (Germany, Italy and Japan) and the Allies (Great Britain, France, the United States and the Soviet Union).
The Second World War began from the attacks of Hitler's Germany, both in revenge for the Treaty of Versailles (in fact it attacked France after having conquered: Belgium, Denmark, Norway, Austria and Czechoslovakia), and for its goal of pan-Germanism.
World War II ended with the defeat of Hitler by the Soviet Union and the United States.
After the Second World War, in fact, the territory of Germany and Berlin was divided into 4 zones, one for France, one for Great Britain, one for the United States and one for the Soviet Union.
